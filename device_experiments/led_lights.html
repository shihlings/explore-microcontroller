LED Lights

Introduction

The LED Light is a General-Purpose Input/Output (GPIO) device, and it is connected to the Raspberry Pi Pico directly through GPIO/PIO ports. Its cathode connects to GND, and its anode needs to be connected to one 330 Ohm resistor, which then connects to one of the GPIO/PIO ports. The 330 Ohm resistor is required because the LED Light can burn out when connecting to a GPIO/PIO output without adding any resistors. The benefit of connecting the LED Light directly to a GPIO/PIO output is that the LED Light will be brighter.

In this test, the program is designed to turn on and off each LED Light. The LED Lights can be useful indicators for all programs.

Demonstration

The program is initiated by the Raspberry Pi 4B (the touch screen in the upper part of the screen), and the Raspberry Pi Pico runs the program.

The program turns on the Red LED Light first and prints "Red LED on" on the Raspberry Pi 4B. Then, it turns off the Red LED Light, turns on the Yellow LED Light, and prints "Yellow LED on" on the Raspberry Pi 4B. Finally, it turns off the Yellow LED Light, turns on the Green LED Light, and prints "Green LED on" on the Raspberry Pi 4B. The program exits after the Green LED Light is turned off after the timer.

No issues were encountered during development and the test. However, the Green LED Light appears to be a lot dimmer than the Yellow and Red LED Lights. To fix this issue, the resistor for the Green LED Light needs to be removed, which comes at the cost of the Green LED Light dying faster.
